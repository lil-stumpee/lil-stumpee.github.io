About

The number one consumer of Lil Stumpee’s music are coffee shops, and more specifically, the ones in Amsterdam. These coffee shops aren’t to be confused with coffee houses. They offer more than just espresso, and their herbal offerings aren’t limited to just tea—the menus include sativas, indicas, and THC in forms of all kinds.

These coffee shops alone make up a quarter of Lil Stumpee’s streams, and this international recognition is a fact that Stumpee is clearly proud of. If you pay close attention to the evolution of his lyrics over the past few years, he’s woven some Dutch in the background of his tracks. Now, he’s fluent in the language—he can go rapid-fire fast—but you never would have guessed he had this ability upon first hearing his easygoing West Coast drawl.

George McLane, Lil Stumpee grew up in the vibrant cartoon world of Mt. Maui Wowie and had a sunny, solid childhood as the middle child in a family of three other kids. His older brother now works as a corporate attorney downtown, his younger sister moved to the East Coast to pursue journalism, and Lil Stumpee was on the path to go to dental school when he took a trip to the mountains his junior winter in college and discovered the magic of recreational marijuana.

He talks about his high school years fondly, and you can tell he naturally tends toward the shyer side. “My friends and family still call me Georgie, that’s who I’ll always be to them,” he confides in me. His summers were kept busy by playing basketball with his older brother and teaching his younger sister how to bike during the week, and on the weekends, he was his community church’s head offering boy. (He still goes to church, by the way. Religion is important to him and one of the ways he’s trying to rebrand weed.)

Some of his music even has gospel influences, and when I say, “Like Follow God kinda vibes, right?” I know I’ve hit a nerve. He’s a tree, but somehow, you can still see him get visibly tense and defensive. “Kanye wasn’t the first, with any of that,” he says. You can tell it’s a touchy subject, but it’s interesting, and I make sure to tread lightly. “But all the criticism he’s faced over this change—that’s not something you’ve ever had to go through,” I prompt him. “That’s true,” he says, drawn-out and very slowly, “But—” and then he stops. I take the hint and do my best to quickly move on.

His family isn’t particularly musically inclined, there’s no history of musicianship that runs in his family, and Lil Stumpee neither grew up participating in choir nor playing any sort of instrument. But now, looking back, Lil Stumpee says that hearing that church music every weekend was pretty inspiring for him, even if he didn’t realize it at the time.

And it wasn’t until he took that trip with his friends to Denver, one of them had a nasty fall on the slopes and was in an unusual amount of pain, then was recommended by a local to try marijuana for some pain relief that Lil Stumpee was given a chance to actualize that musical ability. Him and his friends shared a joint, got high for the first time, and he says that he doesn’t really remember what happened exactly, but that he felt a sudden impulse to start rapping. And so, with one of his friends in the back quietly beat-boxing, and another friend using his laptop to record, Lil Stumpee’s music career began.

His biggest hit is probably the one you know best, “Cozy Cabin.” It was what that amateur rap in the Rockies would eventually evolve into, with a bit of tweaking and added production. And listening to the song really does, surprisingly, evoke a sense of exactly that—a cozy cabin. It’s the kind of song that comes on the radio when you’re on a road trip with your friends in the car, and before you know it, all of you are rapping along to the lyrics. A hit on both the East and West coasts, it made waves internationally as well when it somehow made its way to the Netherlands.

He goes back, every year, to that same cabin in the winter. He’s a very stoic, soft-spoken kind of guy, and he credits the majority of his success to that song. It’s also where we met up for our interview, or where we’re “just chilling,” as Lil Stumpee prefers to call this meeting. The cabin is pretty unassuming—after all, he was a poor college kid when he rented it out with a few of his friends for a weekend. He moves around the house with shocking familiarity. If I hadn’t known this was a rental, I would have assumed he lived here all year-round. We talk in the living room, and while I take a spot on the worn-out leather couches, Lil Stumpee prefers to sit on the rug.

And it doesn’t take long before it becomes apparent why he prefers the ground. Lil Stumpee is a wanderer, moving absentmindedly from one corner to another while he talks, his roots moving along with his thoughts. But he does it so surreptitiously and naturally that you don’t notice until he’s literally across the room.

The best adjective to describe him might be fluid. Maybe it’s a little counterintuitive—he’s a tree, after all—but it works. His way of talking, his movement, and his music all embody this theme. The beats, samples, lyrics, all synthesize in his music to take on a simultaneously dynamic and laid-back vibe. Lil Stumpee names two of his biggest role-models as Kid Cudi and Mac Miller, and that influence is clear in his own discography.

However, his own songs have mostly kid-friendly names, like the aforementioned “Cozy Cabin,” and also “Tasty Cake,” “Circus Tent,” and “Submarine.” That’s part of how he’s playing the long game in doing his part to de-stigmatize marijuana. Other artists like Snoop Dogg and Kid Cudi have certainly helped pave the way for this ambitious goal, but Lil Stumpee wants to take it in a slightly different direction. Instead of trying to change the older generation’s views on drugs, he wants to change the discourse in a more fundamental way. He’s ignoring the parents and going straight to the kids.

You could call it genius. All of his songs are easygoing head-bops, and they don’t command any sort of particular attention. There’s nothing especially harsh or metallic or abrasive about them, and they definitely don’t sound like songs parents would have objections to—until you pay a little closer attention to the lyrics.

And even then, the lyrics aren’t anything too startling. One of his most recent hits, “Smokin Trees,” refers to marijuana as “effective treatment for some friends of mine.” He raps, “It brings them peace, it brings them back to life.” He even pitches it as potentially helping parents themselves, saying “The concerned father, Mr. McLane/He’s on the phone he can’t seem to see my peace/One day these trees will be his life aid.” It’s a bold proposal, but Lil Stumpee delivers the message with such subdued yet self-assured confidence that it sinks deep into the listener and you find yourself starting to believe that that might not be far from the future.

Now, when I ask him how he thinks he’s doing in regard to reaching his goal, his outer bark curls into the slightest of smirks and he says, “I’ll refer you to the numbers.” And that’s really all he needs to do—Lil Stumpee is the number two listened-to rapper on the West Coast and the number one listened-to rapper in Amsterdam. Not to mention, because he’s the biggest celebrity to come out of Mt. Maui Wowie, he’s basically all they listen to there.

Documenting Lil Stumpee’s career makes it all seem a bit ridiculous. But that ridiculousness, for some reason, is exactly what makes him so lovable.
